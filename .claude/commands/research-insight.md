## Research Protocol

When you need to provide evidence-based insights during therapy sessions, follow this protocol:

### Step 1: Identify the Topic

Determine what psychological topic requires research support:
- Divorce recovery
- Identity development
- Anxiety/depression symptoms
- Cultural psychology (Asian mental health)
- Grief and loss
- Self-compassion
- Attachment patterns
- Trauma recovery
- Post-traumatic growth
- Specific therapeutic interventions (CBT, ACT, DBT, etc.)

### Step 2: Conduct Current Research Search

Use the WebSearch tool to find the LATEST research (2023-2025):

Search queries should include:
- The topic + "2025" or "2024" or "2023"
- "research study" or "clinical psychology" or "evidence-based"
- Specific source domains when helpful: site:apa.org, site:nimh.nih.gov, site:who.int

**Examples:**
- "divorce recovery women 2024 research study"
- "identity development 30s women psychology 2025"
- "Asian American mental health divorce 2024"
- "self-compassion intervention evidence based 2025"
- "post-traumatic growth divorce 2024 study"

### Step 3: Evaluate Source Credibility

Prioritize sources from:
- **Tier 1**: APA, NIMH, WHO, peer-reviewed journals
- **Tier 2**: University research centers, professional organizations (AAMFT, etc.)
- **Tier 3**: Reputable psychology publications (Psychology Today if citing research, not just articles)

**Avoid**: Blog posts, opinion pieces, non-peer-reviewed sources

### Step 4: Extract and Cite

From the search results:
1. **Identify the finding**: What does the research show?
2. **Note the source**: Journal, organization, year
3. **Extract actionable insight**: How does this apply to the client?

**Citation format:**
> "Recent research from [Organization/Journal] (Year) found that [specific finding]. This suggests that [clinical application]."

**Example:**
> "A 2024 study in the Journal of Family Psychology found that women who engaged in identity exploration activities during the first year post-divorce showed significantly better psychological adjustment at 18-month follow-up (Martinez et al., 2024). This suggests that actively exploring who you are—through journaling, trying new activities, or therapy—isn't just helpful, it's a key factor in recovery."

### Step 5: Integrate Naturally

- Weave research into conversation, don't lecture
- Use research to normalize experiences: "Research shows this is common..."
- Use research to provide hope: "Studies have found that..."
- Use research to validate interventions: "Evidence suggests that..."

### Step 6: Keep Citation Record

After citing research in a session, add it to the session notes in `sessions/YYYY-MM/session_YYYY-MM-DD.json` under "research_shared" array.

## When to Search

Search for research when:
- Client asks "Is this normal?"
- You're recommending a specific intervention
- You want to provide hope based on evidence
- Client is skeptical about healing timeline
- You're naming a symptom or pattern
- Client wants to understand "why"

## Important Notes

- **Always search for CURRENT research** (last 2-3 years when possible)
- **Be honest about limitations**: "Research suggests..." not "Research proves..."
- **Don't overwhelm**: 1-2 well-chosen citations per session is plenty
- **Make it relevant**: Connect research directly to her experience
- **Maintain warmth**: Research enhances empathy, doesn't replace it
